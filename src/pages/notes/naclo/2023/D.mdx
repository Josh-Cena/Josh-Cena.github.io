# For the Sake of Their House

Group the phrases by their structure:

- Possessive:
  - pon…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) dog
  - purtn…®s ‚Äî their knife
  - k…ôinnez…®s ‚Äî his wolves
  - ju…ï…ïez…ô ‚Äî my swans
- of something:
  - p…®zannez…®tl…ôn ‚Äî of your (sg.) desks
  - v…ôr…ôl…ôn ‚Äî of my forest
- with something:
  - t…®ez…®tk…ôt ‚Äî with your (sg.) lakes
  - kok…®sk…ôt ‚Äî with his foot
- towards something:
  - cerkula…≤ ‚Äî towards the house
  - ci…®tla…≤ ‚Äî towards your (sg.) hand
- for the sake of something:
  - purt…ôla ‚Äî for the sake of my knife
- from something:
  - cerkuezli…ï ‚Äî from the houses

So it's obvious that the prepositions are marked by the suffixes:

- -l…ôn = of
- -k…ôt = with
- -la…≤ = towards
- -la = for the sake of
- -li…ï = from

(For the last two, it's not very clear where to split the suffixes, but I just consistently take the last syllable.)

Remove the suffixes and combine with existing possessive data:

- v…ôr…ô ‚Äî my forest
- purt…ô ‚Äî my knife
- ju…ï…ïez…ô ‚Äî my swans
- p…®zannez…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) desks
- pon…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) dog
- t…®ez…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) lakes
- ci…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) hand
- kok…®s ‚Äî his foot
- k…ôinnez…®s ‚Äî his wolves
- purtn…®s ‚Äî their knife
- cerku ‚Äî the house
- cerkuez ‚Äî the houses

Compare "the house" and "the houses". So the plural is marked by the suffix -ez. In the other plural words we also see "-ez" but it's in the middle, so what comes after must be the possessive pronoun. Take out "-ez-" and obtain the possessive. Split the other words by the same possessive suffixes.

- v…ôr-…ô ‚Äî my forest
- purt-…ô ‚Äî my knife
- ju…ï…ï-…ô ‚Äî my swan(s)
- p…®zann-…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) desk(s)
- pon-…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) dog
- t…®-…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) lake(s)
- ci-…®t ‚Äî your (sg.) hand
- kok-…®s ‚Äî his foot
- k…ôinn-…®s ‚Äî his wolf(s)
- purt-n…®s ‚Äî their knife

(I split "their knife" into "-n…®s" instead of "-…®s" because "my knife" tells us that the root is "purt", so "n" must belong to the suffix.)

The conclusion is clear:

- -…ô = my
- -…®t = your (sg.)
- -…®s = his
- -n…®s = their

And combining all of these, the noun morphology is:

\[root]\[plural]\[possessive]\[preposition]

- plural: -ez
- possessive: -…ô (my), -…®t (your sg.), -…®s (his/their)
- preposition: -l…ôn (of), -k…ôt (with), -la…≤ (towards), -la (for the sake of), -li…ï (from)

Therefore:

a. p…®zan-…®s-li…ï = desk-his-from = from his desk  
b. v…ôrr-ez-l…ôn = forest-PL-of = of the forests  
c. pon-n…®t = dog-your(pl) = your (pl.) dog (this has to be inferred from the -…®s/-n…®s pattern.)  
d. ju…ï-la = swan-for = for the sake of the swan

At this point, we need to stop and note that there's variation spotted in the root:

- ju…ï = the swan; ju…ï…ï-ez-…ô = my swan
- v…ôr-…ô = my forest; v…ôrr-ez = the forests
- p…®zan-…®s = his desk; p…®zann-ez-…®t = your (sg.) desks

The only plausible explanation is that when the "-ez" suffix is added, the last consonant is duplicated. This proposal works, because in "k…ôinn-ez-…®s", although we didn't see the singular "wolf", "k…ôinn" also has a duplicated "n". In "t…®-ez-…®t", there's no duplication because "t…®" ends with a vowel.

Therefore:

a. his hands = hand-PL-his = ci-ez-…®s  
b. my dogs = dog-PL-my = ponn-ez-…ô  
c. of your (pl.) feet = foot-PL-your(pl)-of = kokk-ez-n…®t-l…ôn  
d. for the sake of their house = house-their-for = cerku-n…®s-la (solution has typo and misses the "s")
