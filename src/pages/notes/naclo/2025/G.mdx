The way to approach a phrase translation problem like this is start by making a "template" for the sentences. Here, all sentences are in past tense. All 12 but 2 of them are declarative; the other 2 are interrogative. Most of them are of the form (including the questions):

[Modifier] [Subject] [Verb] [Modifier] [Object]

Where the modifier can be a possessor or an adjective (good, bad, big, little, white, long).

Compare the three sentences that all have "saw". The only common part is "amwi", which is "bamwi" in 1, "amwi" in 11, and "nemwi" in 12. Similarly, in the two sentences involving "eat", the common part is "la", which is "ala" in 6 and "ila" in 8, and we also see "ola" in 4.

8 and 12 both involve "peanut", and the only common part is "cher", which is "ncher" in 8 and "lecher" in 12. This suggests that the parts after them, "i bang" and "lenini", mean "the children's" and "big", respectively. Indeed, "lenini" also appears in 6, and "benini" appears 1, both sentences containing "big". From 1, "bebil" therefore means "fields", so in 10 "kebil" means "field".

At this point, we know that this language is SVO, with the head noun before the modifier. So we can fill out the glosses that just have adjective-noun:

- In 1, "banka" = "women", "balülü" = "good".
- In 2, "banjir" = "men", "babibə" = "bad", "badzo" = "kill", "ibat" = "duck", "lelülü" = "good" (consistent with "balülü").
- In 8, "nko" = "chickens", "ibibə" = "bad" (consistent with "babibə"). Therefore in 7, "nejo" = "hear", "ne bang" = "the children's" (similar to previous "i bang"), and "munkar" = "woman".
- In 9, "ntaw" = "goats", "ilülü" = "good". Therefore in 10, "i banka" = "the women's". In 9, this leaves "bachunga" = "buy", "bande" = "white men".
- In 12, "ngombe" = "cow", "nekikə" = "little".

At this point, we have enough confidence to claim that possessives are formed by appending some possessive marker (i, ke, be, ...) and then the possessor noun, unchanged.

- In 3, "ne banjir" = "the men's" (consistent with previous "banjir"), "kejo" = "hear" (similar to "nejo"), "keto" = "snake", and "ketita" = "long".
- In 5, "ne banka" = "the women's", "mva" = "dog".
- In 6, "le ibat" = "the duck's", "ipe" = "wing".
- In 10, "ke munde" = "the white man's".
- In 11, "be bande" = "the white men's", so "bena" = "things".

Finally in 4, "mabat" = "ducks", "masinxunə" = "want to", "mokan" = "book", "mo munjir" = "the man's".

To summarize, here's the glossary:

- Nouns:
  - woman/women: munkar/banka
  - man/men: munjir/banjir
  - white man/white men: munde/bande
  - children: bang
  - field/fields: kebil/bebil
  - things: bena
  - snake: keto
  - duck/ducks: ibat/mabat
  - wing: ipe
  - goat/goats: ntaw
  - chicken/chickens: nko
  - cow: ngombe
  - dog: mva
  - peanut/peanuts: lecher/ncher
  - book: mokan
- Verbs:
  - see: bamwi (women-fields)/amwi (who-things)/nemwi (cow-peanut)
  - hear: kejo (snake-goat)/nejo (woman-chickens)
  - eat: ola (want to)/ala (who-wing)/ila (chickens-peanuts)
  - want to: masinxunə (ducks)
  - learn to: nejao (dog)
  - read: otang (learn to)
  - buy: bachunga (white men-goats)
  - go to: ikwi o (goats)
- Adjectives:
  - good: lelülü (duck)/ilülü (goats)/balülü (women)
  - bad: ibibə (chickens)/babibə (men)
  - big: lenini (duck/peanut)/benini (fields)
  - little: nekikə (dog/cow)
  - long: ketita (snake)
- Possessive marker:
  - ne (men's goat, women's dog, children's chicken)
  - i (children's peanuts, women's goats)
  - mo (man's book)
  - le (duck's wing)
  - ke (white man's field)
  - be (white men's things)

So we observe that nouns have different prefixes denoting different classes. The prefix participates in pluralization, adjective agreement, verb agreement with subject, and possessive marker with the possessed (part of the table filled in by G1):

| Class    | Noun prefix | Adjective & Possessive | Verb      |
| -------- | ----------- | ---------------------- | --------- |
| human    | mu- / ba-   | mu- / ba-              | ne- / ba- |
| wing     | i- / ma-    | le- / ?                | ? / ma-   |
| domestic | n- / n-[^1] | ne- / i-               | ne- / i-  |
| peanut   | le- / n-    | le- / i-               | ?         |
| book     | mo- / me-   | mo- / me-              | ?         |
| other    | ke- / be-   | ke- / be-              | ke- / be- |

[^1]: "Dog" is special case because it starts with "m" but is in the "n-" class.

In G3, the new observation is that numbers also agree with the head noun, using what appears to be the adjective agreement rules.

a. "pigs" is "nchungə", so it's from the "domestic" class whose singular is still "nchungə". "One" becomes "nemo" from "lemo".  
b. "trees" is "mete", so it's from the "book" class whose singular is "mote". "One" becomes "momo".  
c. "bones" is "befwa", so it's from the "other" class whose singular is "kefwa". "One" becomes "kemo".  
d. "shoulder" is "ipyao", so it's from the "wing" class whose plural is "mapyao". "Two" becomes "mapə" from "ipə".  
e. "finger" is "ledze", so it's from the "peanut" class whose plural is "ndze". "Three" becomes "isa" from "besa".
