# Who Saw the Bear?

{/* cSpell:ignore Balık yüzdü kaplan gibi kaplumbağa için atladı Zürafa koştu gördü zürafayı kurbağayı kurbağa kimi martı balina */}

Clearly "kaplan gibi" = "like a tiger". Furthermore, from:

<Ex>
  <Ex.Line translation>Kaplan kaplumbağa için atladı.</Ex.Line>
  <Ex.Line translation>_The tiger jumped for the turtle._</Ex.Line>
</Ex>

Which also contains "kaplan", and contains the word "tiger" but not "like", we know that "kaplan" = "tiger" and "gibi" = "like". Therefore in Turkish, the word order is as follows:

[subject] [indirect object] [preposition] [verb]

- [subject]/[indirect object]: no case marking on the indirect object; takes the exact same form as the subject
  - balık = fish
  - zürafa = giraffe
  - fil = elephant
  - kaplan = tiger
  - kaplumbağa = turtle
  - ayı = bear
- [preposition]
  - için = for
  - gibi = like
- [verb]
  - yüzdü = swam
  - atladı = jumped
  - koştu = ran

The translations are straightforward by plugging in the words:

In B2, all sentences are variations of "A saw B", including wh-questions of the form "Who saw B?" and "Whom did A see?". We can easily identify the common parts for each structure.

- A saw B. _A B gördü._
  - A is the same subject as before.
  - B has an extra marker. Compare for example:
    - giraffe: "zürafayı" in direct object; "zürafa" otherwise
    - frog: "kurbağayı" in direct object; "kurbağa" otherwise

    Therefore, this extra marker is "-yı". (This question is nice enough that there's _only_ this marker, with no vowel harmony.)

- Who saw B? _Kim B gördü?_
  - Again, B has the "-yı" marker.
- Whom did A see? _A kimi gördü?_
  - So "kim" is "who" in subject and "kimi" is "who" in direct object, which is consistent with the "-yı" marker for direct objects.

We also know a few more words:

- kurbağa = frog
- martı = seagull
- arı = bee
- balina = whale
